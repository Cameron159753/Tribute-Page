Desmond Tutu's Early Child Hood

Desmond Mpilo Tutu was born on 7 October 1931 in Klerksdorp, a city in the northwest South Africa. Around 1941, Tutu's mother moved to Witwatersrand to work as a cook at Ezenzeleni, an institute for the blind in western Johannesburg. Tutu joined her in the city, first living with an aunt. In Johannesburg, he attended a Methodist primary school before transferring to the Swedish Boarding School (SBS). Several months later, he moved with his fatherto Errmelo eatern transvaal. After six months they returned to live with the rest of the family, where Tutu resuming his studies at SBS. He puresed his interest in Christianity and at the age of 12 he underwent confirmation at St Mary's church. ...

Tutu failed the arithmetic component of his primary school exam, but despite this, his father secured him entry to the Johannesburg Bantu High school in 1945, where he excelled academically. There, he joined a rugby team, developing a lifelong love of the sport. Outside of school, he earned money selling oranges and as a working as a caddie for white golfers To avoid the expense of a daily trsin commute to school, he briefly lived with family near Johannesburg, before moving back in with his parents. He then returned to Johannesburg by moving into a hostel that was part of the Anglican complex surrounding the Church of Christ the King in Sophiatown. He became a server at the church and came under the influence of its priest. In 1947 Tutu contracted TB and was hospitalised for 18 months. He retured to school in 1949 and took his national exams in late 1950, gaining a second-class pass.

College and teaching career

Wanting to become a docter, Desmond Tutu secured admission to study medicine at the University of the Witwatersrand, however his parents could not afford the tuition fees. Instead, he turned toward teaching, gaining a government scholarship to start a course at Pretoria in 1951. He helped to organise the Literacy and Dramatic Society, and chaired the Cultural and Debating Society for two years.
